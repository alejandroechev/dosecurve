What is a Dose-Response Curve?

A dose-response curve describes the relationship between the concentration of a drug (or other compound) and the magnitude of the biological effect it produces. This is one of the most fundamental concepts in pharmacology, toxicology, and drug discovery.

As the drug concentration increases from very low to very high, the response typically follows a characteristic sigmoidal (S-shaped) pattern when plotted on a logarithmic dose axis:

  • At low concentrations — the response is near its baseline (no drug effect).
  • At intermediate concentrations — the response changes steeply as receptors become increasingly occupied.
  • At high concentrations — the response plateaus as all available receptors are saturated.

This sigmoidal shape arises from the underlying receptor-ligand binding equilibrium. The steepness and position of the curve encode critical pharmacological information about the compound's potency and mechanism of action.

IC50 and Hill Slope

IC50 — The Half-Maximal Inhibitory Concentration

The IC50 is the concentration of a compound needed to inhibit the biological response by 50% of its maximal effect. It is the single most commonly reported metric in dose-response analysis and serves as a standard measure of drug potency.

A lower IC50 means the drug is more potent — it achieves half-maximal effect at a smaller concentration. Note that potency (IC50) is distinct from efficacy, which refers to the maximum effect a drug can achieve regardless of dose.

Hill Slope (Hill Coefficient)

The Hill slope (also called the Hill coefficient, n) describes the steepness of the dose-response curve at the IC50 point. It reflects the cooperativity of ligand binding:

Hill SlopeInterpretation
n = 1Standard mass-action binding; no cooperativity
n > 1Positive cooperativity (steeper curve); binding of one ligand facilitates the next
n < 1Negative cooperativity (shallower curve); or heterogeneous receptor populations

Most single-site receptor interactions produce a Hill slope near 1.0. Deviations suggest multi-site interactions, multiple receptor subtypes, or non-specific effects.

The 4-Parameter Logistic (4PL) Model

The 4PL equation is the gold-standard model for fitting sigmoidal dose-response data:

y = Bottom + (Top − Bottom) / (1 + (x / IC50)n)

Each of the four parameters has a clear biological or practical meaning:

ParameterMeaning
BottomResponse at zero drug concentration (baseline). For inhibition curves, this is the maximum uninhibited response.
TopResponse at infinite drug concentration (full effect plateau). For inhibition, this approaches the fully inhibited level.
IC50Concentration producing a response halfway between Top and Bottom.
n (Hill slope)Steepness of the transition region. Controls how sharply the curve transitions between plateaus.

Why 4PL instead of 3PL?

A 3-parameter model fixes the Bottom at 0 (or Top at 100%), assuming you know the baseline exactly. The 4PL model lets both plateaus float, which is more robust when:

  • The assay baseline isn't exactly zero
  • Maximum inhibition doesn't reach 100%
  • There is non-specific binding or background signal

For most real-world assays, the 4PL model produces better fits and more accurate IC50 estimates.

Curve Fitting

Levenberg-Marquardt Optimization

DoseCurve uses the Levenberg-Marquardt (L-M) algorithm to fit the 4PL model to your data. L-M is an iterative nonlinear least-squares optimizer that blends gradient descent (stable but slow) with Gauss-Newton (fast but unstable), adapting its strategy at each step:

Δp = (JTJ + λI)−1 JTr

Where J is the Jacobian matrix of partial derivatives, r is the residual vector, and λ is the damping parameter. When λ is large, the algorithm behaves like gradient descent; when small, it behaves like Gauss-Newton.

R² — Goodness of Fit

The coefficient of determination (R²) measures how well the fitted curve explains the variance in your data:

R² = 1 − SSres / SStot

An R² close to 1.0 indicates an excellent fit. Values below 0.95 may suggest the data doesn't follow a standard sigmoidal pattern, or there are outliers that need attention.

95% Confidence Intervals

DoseCurve computes 95% confidence intervals for each fitted parameter using the Jacobian-based covariance matrix approach:

  1. Compute the Jacobian J at the best-fit parameters
  2. Estimate the covariance matrix: Cov = s² × (JTJ)−1
  3. Standard errors are the square roots of the diagonal of Cov
  4. 95% CI = parameter ± t0.025 × SE

The CI for IC50 is especially important — a wide CI signals that the IC50 estimate is uncertain, possibly because data points are sparse near the inflection point.

Practical Considerations

Serial Dilutions

Dose-response experiments typically use serial dilutions — each successive concentration is a fixed multiple (e.g., 3× or 10×) of the previous one. This produces evenly spaced points on the log-dose axis, which is essential for adequately sampling both plateaus and the steep transition region.

Log-Dose Axis

Concentrations are always plotted on a logarithmic scale. On a linear scale, the sigmoidal curve would appear as a step function with most points compressed at the left. The log scale reveals the full sigmoidal shape and allows visual comparison of IC50 values.

Normalization

Raw assay readings (absorbance, fluorescence, luminescence) are often normalized to percent activity or percent inhibition. Common approaches:

  • % Inhibition = 100 × (1 − (sample − blank) / (control − blank))
  • % Activity = 100 × (sample − blank) / (control − blank)

Normalization ensures that Top and Bottom parameters have intuitive meaning (e.g., 100% and 0%) and makes results comparable across experiments.

Replicate Handling

Running replicates (typically 2–4 per concentration) is critical for statistical confidence. DoseCurve automatically:

  • Detects replicate columns in pasted data
  • Computes the mean and SEM (standard error of the mean) for each concentration
  • Displays error bars on the chart
  • Fits the curve to the mean values

How to Use DoseCurve

1 Paste your data — Enter concentration-response data into the text area. Use tab-separated columns: the first column is concentration, and subsequent columns are response replicates. Include a header row.

2 Click "Fit Curve" — DoseCurve will parse your data, compute mean ± SEM for replicates, and fit the 4PL model using Levenberg-Marquardt optimization.

3 Review results — The results panel shows IC50 (with 95% CI), Hill slope, Top, Bottom, and R². The chart displays your data points with error bars and the fitted sigmoidal curve with an IC50 annotation line.

4 Export — Download the chart as PNG or SVG for publications, or export the full results (fitted parameters + curve data) as a CSV file.

Glossary

IC50 — Half-maximal inhibitory concentration. The drug concentration that reduces the response to 50% of its maximum.

EC50 — Half-maximal effective concentration. Similar to IC50 but used for stimulatory (agonist) dose-response curves.

Hill Slope — The steepness coefficient of the dose-response curve. Also called the Hill coefficient (n). Reflects binding cooperativity.

4PL — 4-Parameter Logistic model. The standard sigmoidal equation with Bottom, Top, IC50, and Hill slope as free parameters.

Levenberg-Marquardt — An iterative nonlinear least-squares optimization algorithm that blends gradient descent and Gauss-Newton methods.

R² — Coefficient of determination. Fraction of data variance explained by the model, ranging from 0 (no fit) to 1 (perfect fit).

Confidence Interval — A range of values within which the true parameter value is expected to lie with a given probability (typically 95%).

SEM — Standard Error of the Mean. SEM = SD / √n, quantifying the precision of the mean estimate from replicates.
